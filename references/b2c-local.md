# B2C Local Service Audit Reference

For businesses selling directly to consumers in a defined geographic market.
Examples: junk removal, HVAC, roofing, pool service, pressure washing, landscaping.

---

## Business model characteristics

- **Customer**: Homeowners, renters, small business owners
- **Sales motion**: On-demand or scheduled, one-time or recurring
- **Decision speed**: Fast (same-day or next-day booking common)
- **Key trust signals**: Reviews, real job photos, licensing/insurance, response time
- **Primary conversion**: Phone call or quote form

---

## Competitive landscape — what to look for

### National franchise players
Always present in major markets. Research:
- 1-800-GOT-JUNK?, Junk King, ServiceMaster, Angi-affiliated brands, etc.
- They dominate brand awareness but lack local authenticity
- Generic city page templates — opportunity for genuinely local content
- Premium pricing — opportunity for transparent pricing differentiator

### Local operators
The real competition. Look for:
- Google Business Profile review volume and rating
- Quality of website (Wix vs. custom vs. Webflow)
- Whether they publish pricing (rare, high value if you do)
- Neighborhood-level content
- Social media presence and recency

### What to document per competitor
- Name, URL, city focus
- Review count + average rating (Google, Yelp)
- Services listed
- Pricing: published / estimate-only / hidden
- Location/neighborhood pages: yes/no/count
- Blog: active / inactive / none
- App or booking technology
- Differentiator (eco, same-day, pricing, charity, etc.)

## Keyword tiers for B2C local

### Tier 1: Primary core
Format: `[service] [city]` / `[service] [city] TX`
- High competition, essential to rank for
- Target: homepage, primary service pages

### Tier 2: Neighborhood / local intent
Format: `[service] [neighborhood]`
- Lower competition than city-wide, higher conversion intent
- Testimonials citing neighborhood names = free content seeds
- Target: dedicated neighborhood/location pages
- Example: "junk removal Oak Lawn", "HVAC repair Deep Ellum"

### Tier 3: Long-tail high-intent
Format: modifiers + service + location
- "same day [service] [city]"
- "cheap [service] [city]"
- "[specific item] removal [city]" (hot tub, mattress, piano)
- "[situation] cleanout [city]" (estate, hoarder, foreclosure)
- High conversion — user has specific need, ready to book

### Tier 4: Informational / blog
- "how much does [service] cost"
- "[service] vs [alternative]" (junk removal vs dumpster rental)
- "what can [service] companies take"
- "how to dispose of [item]"
- Builds topical authority, captures early-funnel traffic

## Technical audit priorities for B2C local

### Highest impact
1. **Google Business Profile** — often more important than the website for local B2C
   - Claim if unclaimed
   - Complete all fields: categories, services, hours, photos, description
   - Review generation strategy — volume matters
2. **LocalBusiness schema** — name, address, phone, geo, hours, service area
3. **Review schema** — mark up testimonials
4. **NAP consistency** — Name, Address, Phone identical across site, GBP, and directories

### Common B2C-specific fails
- Placeholder phone numbers (555 numbers, tel: href mismatch)
- Stock photos instead of real job photos (massive trust gap)
- Stat counters showing 0 (looks abandoned)
- Dead CTA buttons ("SCHEDULE NOW", "GET QUOTE" linking to #)
- No About page (who are these people?)
- Testimonials with no source link (appear fabricated)

### Location page strategy
Build individual pages for:
- Each neighborhood with testimonial or job history evidence
- Adjacent cities within service radius
- High-value suburbs (often less competitive than city center)

Page URL format: `/locations/[neighborhood]/` or `/[city]/[neighborhood]-junk-removal/`
Each page needs: unique content (not a template), embedded map, local phone number if possible

## Content strategy for B2C local

### Core pages (must exist at launch)
- Homepage
- About (who you are, founded when, licensed/insured, service area)
- All service pages (one per service, not one page with anchors)
- Pricing (rare in market = massive differentiator)
- Contact / Get a Quote
- Service area (or location pages hub)

### Top blog post types that work for B2C local
1. Cost guide: "How Much Does [Service] Cost in [City]? [Year] Pricing"
2. Comparison: "[Service] vs [Alternative]: Which Is Right for Your Project?"
3. What we take/don't take: "What Can [Service Company] Remove? (And What We Can't)"
4. Process explainer: "How Same-Day [Service] Works — From Booking to Done"
5. Situation guide: "How to [Handle Situation]: A Step-by-Step Guide" (estate cleanout, etc.)
6. Eco angle: "Where Does Your [Junk/Waste] Actually Go? Our Recycling Process"
7. Contractor B2B: "[Service] for [City] Contractors — Commercial Cleanup Made Simple"
8. Neighborhood feature: "[Neighborhood], [City]: [Company] in Your Area"
9. FAQ: "Your Top Questions About [Service] — Answered"
10. Seasonal: "Spring Cleanout Season in [City]: What to Haul Before [X]"

## Strategic opportunities unique to B2C local

Check for these before writing the "3 strategic opportunities" section:

1. **Neighborhood gap** — Do any competitors have genuine neighborhood pages?
   If no: build them. Testimonials citing neighborhoods = built-in content seed.

2. **Pricing transparency** — Does anyone publish pricing?
   If no: publishing a pricing guide is a major differentiator (rare in most local service markets).

3. **Review gap** — Are national franchises outranking local operators despite worse reviews?
   If yes: aggressive review generation + local personality content can flip rankings.

4. **Eco angle** — Does anyone own the recycling/sustainability angle authentically?
   If no and client has an eco process: build content around it.

5. **Same-day / response time** — Does anyone market this prominently?
   If no: if client offers it, front-and-center the guarantee.

6. **Real photos vs. stock** — Are competitors using stock photography?
   If yes: real job photos are an immediate trust differentiator.
