# B2C Local Service Audit Reference

For businesses selling directly to consumers in a defined geographic market.
Examples: any trade, service, or professional that serves consumers in a defined geographic area.

---

## Business model characteristics

- **Customer**: Homeowners, renters, small business owners
- **Sales motion**: On-demand or scheduled, one-time or recurring
- **Decision speed**: Fast (same-day or next-day booking common)
- **Key trust signals**: Reviews, real job photos, licensing/insurance, response time
- **Primary conversion**: Phone call or quote form

---

## Competitive landscape — what to look for

### National franchise players
Research whether national or franchise brands are present in this market. They often dominate
brand awareness but may have weaknesses worth verifying:
- Do their city pages have genuine local content or are they templated copy?
- Is their pricing higher than local operators? (verify — don't assume)
- Do they rank well but have poor reviews? (check actual star ratings)
- Are they missing neighborhood-level targeting?

These are patterns to investigate, not assumptions to carry in. Confirm each one is
actually true for this market before using it as a strategic angle.

### Local operators
The real competition for most clients. Document:
- Google Business Profile review volume and rating
- Website quality and technology (Wix vs. custom vs. Webflow)
- Whether they publish pricing (rare, high value if the client does)
- Neighborhood-level content existence
- Social media presence and recency

### What to document per competitor
- Name, URL, city focus
- Review count + average rating (Google, Yelp) — pull current numbers
- Services listed
- Pricing: published / estimate-only / hidden
- Location/neighborhood pages: yes/no/count
- Blog: active / inactive / none
- Differentiator (eco, same-day, pricing, financing, etc.)

## Keyword strategy for B2C local

See `keywords.md` for the full 4-tier framework. B2C-specific notes:

- **Tier 1** targets the homepage and primary service pages with city-level terms
- **Tier 2** is where B2C local wins — neighborhood pages that national franchises can't match
- **Tier 3** should be built around specific situations, not just service + city.
  Think about what the customer has in front of them right now — a specific problem that needs
  solving today. That urgency and specificity is your Tier 3 keyword.
- **Tier 4** blog content builds topical authority and captures buyers earlier in the journey

Seed Tier 2 from testimonials on the live site — neighborhood names mentioned by real customers
are both keyword candidates and location page content seeds.

## Technical audit priorities for B2C local

### Highest impact
1. **Google Business Profile** — often more important than the website for local B2C
   - Claim if unclaimed
   - Complete all fields: categories, services, hours, photos, description
   - Review generation strategy — volume matters for map pack ranking
2. **LocalBusiness schema** — name, address, phone, geo, hours, service area
3. **Review schema** — mark up testimonials on the site
4. **NAP consistency** — Name, Address, Phone identical across site, GBP, and directories

### Common B2C-specific fails
- Placeholder phone numbers (tel: href mismatch with displayed number)
- Stock photos instead of real job photos — major trust gap for service businesses
- Stat counters showing 0 (JS not loading)
- Dead CTA buttons linking to `#`
- No About page
- Testimonials with no source or date (appear fabricated)

### Location page strategy
Build individual pages for:
- Each neighborhood with testimonial or job history evidence
- Adjacent cities within service radius
- High-value suburbs (often less competitive than city center)

Each page needs: unique content (not a template), embedded map, local phone number if possible.
URL format: `/locations/[area]/` or `/[area]-[service]/` — use whichever fits the site architecture and matches how searchers phrase the query.

## Google Business Profile strategy

GBP is often more important than the website for B2C local. The map pack appears above
organic results for most local service queries.

### GBP competitive analysis
Before making recommendations, audit what competitors are doing:
- How many posts have they published in the last 90 days?
- What post types are they using (offers, updates, events, products)?
- What CTAs do they use (call now, book online, learn more)?
- Are they targeting specific neighborhoods or landmarks in post copy?
- What's their review velocity — how many new reviews per month?
- Are they responding to reviews (good signal) or ignoring them?

Identify what patterns correlate with strong map rankings — consistency and recency
matter more than post quality for most local service categories.

### GBP posting plan output
Based on the competitive analysis, produce a concrete posting plan:
- Post frequency (specific number per week/month — not "regularly")
- Post type mix (offers vs. updates vs. service highlights)
- CTA approach (what action to drive and why)
- Local content angles (specific neighborhoods, landmarks, seasonal hooks)
- Review generation recommendation (when and how to ask)

Avoid vague advice. "Post more often" is not a recommendation. "Post 2x per week —
one service highlight with a specific neighborhood and a Call Now CTA, one seasonal
or local-angle update" is a recommendation.

## Content strategy for B2C local

### Core pages (must exist at launch)
- Homepage
- About (who you are, founded when, licensed/insured, service area)
- One page per service — not one page with anchor links
- Pricing (rare in market = major differentiator if client will do it)
- Contact / Get a Quote
- Location pages hub or individual suburb pages

### Blog post types that work for B2C local
1. Cost guide: "How Much Does [Service] Cost in [City]? [Year] Pricing"
2. Comparison: "[Service] vs [Alternative]: Which Is Right for You?"
3. Process explainer: "How [Service] Works — From Booking to Done"
4. Situation guide: "What to Do When [Specific Problem Situation]"
5. Seasonal or local hook: "[Seasonal angle] [Service] in [City]"
6. Neighborhood feature: "[Service] in [Neighborhood], [City]"
7. FAQ: "Your Questions About [Service] — Answered"
8. Trust builder: "How to Choose a [Service] Company (And What to Avoid)"

## Strategic opportunities for B2C local

Before writing the 3 strategic opportunities, check each of these. Only use the ones
that are actually true for this client and market — verified during research, not assumed.

1. **Neighborhood content gap** — Do any competitors have genuine neighborhood pages?
   If no: location pages seeded from testimonials is an uncontested opportunity.

2. **Pricing transparency** — Does anyone publish pricing?
   If no: a pricing guide is a major differentiator. Confirm client is willing to publish
   before recommending it.

3. **Review gap** — Are competitors ranking well despite poor review scores?
   If yes: aggressive review generation + local personality content can flip rankings.
   Check actual current ratings before using this angle.

4. **Same-day / response time** — Does anyone market this prominently?
   If no and client offers it: this is a differentiator worth a landing page and GBP posts.

5. **Real photos vs. stock** — Are competitors using generic stock photography?
   If yes: real job photos are an immediate trust differentiator. Easy win.

6. **Content gap** — What questions are competitors' sites failing to answer?
   Look at their service pages and blogs: what's missing? What does a buyer need to know
   before calling that nobody is explaining well? That gap is a content opportunity.
